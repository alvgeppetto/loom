The Problem: Diagnostic Tests Targeting the Wrong Part of the Virus

From the brief — Introduction
CRISPR-based detection platforms have proven rapid pathogen detection, but guide RNA design follows historical RT-PCR conventions: structural and surface genes predominate. Immune-exposed genes accumulate mutations under selection pressure, degrading diagnostic sensitivity — as demonstrated when SHERLOCK_S targeting the SARS-CoV-2 Spike degraded to 63% conservation across 9.2 million genomes.

In plain English: CRISPR diagnostic tests work by designing tiny molecular probes (guide RNAs) that latch onto a specific stretch of the virus's genetic code. When there's a match, the test lights up positive.

The problem is that most tests target the virus's outer shell — the Spike protein, the Nucleocapsid — because that's where traditional PCR tests aimed. But the immune system attacks those same parts, so the virus mutates there fastest. When Spike mutated (D614G and beyond), the SHERLOCK_S test designed against it lost a third of its targets.

🔑
Analogy: A burglar alarm that only triggers when the burglar wears a red jacket. Once the burglar switches to blue, silence. This paper targets the burglar's fingerprints — internal machinery so essential that any change would kill the virus itself.

The 52 Targets: Replication Machinery That Can't Mutate

From the brief — Results
From 29,640 candidate windows, 183 carry compatible PAM sites across 89 genomic regions. After excluding regions overlapping published diagnostic targets (N, E, S, nsp12/RdRp), 52 truly novel regions remain — all ≥8 mismatches from any of 83 published guide sequences.

What nsp13, nsp14, ORF1a are: These are the virus's internal copying machinery. Nsp13 (helicase) unzips the genetic code for copying. Nsp14 (ExoN) proofreads copies for errors. ORF1a encodes most of the core replication proteins. Without any of these, the virus cannot reproduce — so they're under intense evolutionary pressure to stay exactly as they are.

What "99.2% conservation" means: Out of 9.2 million known SARS-CoV-2 genomes collected worldwide, this exact 20-letter DNA sequence appears, letter-perfect, in 99.2% of them. The best existing diagnostic guide (DETECTR_E for the E gene) scores 98.34%. These new targets match or beat that benchmark.

Zero human genome matches: Every candidate was checked against the complete 3.2-billion-letter human reference genome. None appear anywhere in human DNA, so a test using them won't false-positive on the patient's own cells.

A 130,000-Target Database Across 12 Pathogens

From the brief — Results
Across 12 pathogens plus a panviral reference, the database contains ~130,000 conservation-ranked CRISPR candidate sequences (top 10,000 per pathogen), of which 8,113 carry SpCas9 NGG PAMs.

What this database is: For each of 12 human pathogens — from HIV to Mpox to cholera — the pipeline identified the 10,000 most-conserved genetic addresses suitable for CRISPR targeting. Think of it as a phonebook of the best places to aim a diagnostic test for each pathogen.

Important caveat: Corpus sizes vary enormously — 9.2 million genomes for SARS-CoV-2 but only 382 for cholera. A target that's "95% conserved across 382 genomes" carries much less statistical weight than one conserved across 9.2 million. Conservation numbers are not directly comparable across pathogens.

What This Study Cannot Tell You

From the brief — Limitations
Conservation ≠ efficacy. All 52 candidates require wet-lab validation; k-mer conservation cannot predict cleavage efficiency, binding kinetics, or limit of detection. In silico only. No experimental validation was performed.

The bottom line: Everything in this paper was done by computer. No virus was grown in a lab, no CRISPR guide was physically synthesized, no diagnostic test was actually run. Computational predictions can be wrong.

Finding a highly conserved sequence is a necessary first step but not proof that a diagnostic built around it will work in a real clinical sample. RNA folding, enzyme binding kinetics, reaction chemistry, and real-world sample conditions all matter — and none can be predicted from sequence data alone.

Other caveats: the SARS-CoV-2 corpus is 65% Omicron-era sequences (it reflects what was circulating, not uniform variant sampling); the multi-pathogen datasets range from 382 cholera genomes to 9.2M SARS-CoV-2; and the literature scan is dated March 2026 — new papers may have appeared since.

These are leads — very promising leads — but leads.
